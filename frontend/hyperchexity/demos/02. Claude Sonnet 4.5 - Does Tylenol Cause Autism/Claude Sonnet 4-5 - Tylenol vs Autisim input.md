## What the Research Shows

The scientific consensus is that no studies have established a causal relationship between acetaminophen use and autism, despite some observational studies reporting associations. The key distinction here is between *association* (correlation) and *causation*.

### Studies Showing Associations

A 2025 Mount Sinai systematic review of 46 studies found that prenatal acetaminophen exposure may be associated with increased risk of autism and ADHD. Multiple large-scale cohort studies, including the Nurses' Health Study II and Boston Birth Cohort, have reported this association.

### Studies Showing No Causal Effect

A major Swedish study by Brian Lee at Drexel University initially found a small association in the general population, but when comparing siblings within the same family where the mother took acetaminophen for one pregnancy and not another, the increased risk completely disappeared. A 2024 JAMA study analyzing 2.5 million Swedish children found no association between acetaminophen use during pregnancy and increased risk of autism, ADHD, or intellectual disability when controlling for confounding factors.

## Why the Conflicting Results?

Confounding factors complicate the research—women may take acetaminophen to address health conditions like infections or fevers that themselves may be linked to autism, and genetics plays a major role in autism development.

## Current Recommendations

The FDA initiated a label change process in 2025 to reflect evidence of a possible association, while noting that acetaminophen remains the only over-the-counter drug approved for treating fevers during pregnancy. Health experts recommend judicious use—lowest effective dose for the shortest duration under medical guidance—rather than complete avoidance.

**Bottom line**: The evidence suggests possible associations but does not prove causation. Pregnant women should consult their healthcare providers rather than stopping medication, as untreated pain and fever can also harm fetal development.
