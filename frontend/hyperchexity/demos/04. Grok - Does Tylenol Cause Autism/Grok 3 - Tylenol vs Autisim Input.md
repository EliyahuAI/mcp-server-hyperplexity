Prompt: Does Tylenol cause Autism? Include references and explicit citations references numerically in the text.

No, Tylenol (acetaminophen) does not cause autism. This claim originated from observational studies showing statistical associations between prenatal or early childhood exposure to acetaminophen and increased autism spectrum disorder (ASD) risk, but these associations do not prove causation. Major health organizations, including the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) and the FDA, have reviewed the evidence and concluded that acetaminophen remains safe and recommended for pain/fever relief during pregnancy when used as directed, with no established causal link to ASD.

### Key Evidence Against Causation
1. **Consensus statements from medical bodies**: In 2021, a group of studies prompted a "consensus statement" signed by some scientists urging precaution with acetaminophen use in pregnancy, citing ~26 observational studies showing associations (odds ratios typically 1.2–3.0). However, ACOG responded in 2021 that these studies are limited by confounding (e.g., parents may give acetaminophen for fevers/infections that independently raise neurodevelopmental risks), recall bias, and lack of biological plausibility for a direct causal mechanism. ACOG recommends continued use as the safest analgesic in pregnancy.^[1]^

2. **Systematic reviews and meta-analyses**: A 2021 meta-analysis of 7 cohort studies (n>130,000 children) found an adjusted odds ratio of 1.19 (95% CI: 1.14–1.25) for ASD with prenatal acetaminophen exposure, but authors noted high heterogeneity (I²=72%) and emphasized confounding by indication (e.g., maternal fever or infection).^[2]^ A 2023 umbrella review of 16 meta-analyses on neurodevelopmental outcomes concluded evidence for causation is "very low" per GRADE criteria due to inconsistency and bias risks.^[3]^

3. **Biological plausibility issues**: Proposed mechanisms (e.g., endocannabinoid disruption or oxidative stress) lack direct evidence in humans at therapeutic doses. Animal studies use supra-therapeutic exposures not comparable to human use.^[4]^

4. **Litigation-driven misinformation**: Over 100 U.S. lawsuits (as of 2025) allege acetaminophen causes ASD, relying on the same observational data. In 2023, a federal judge ruled much of the plaintiffs' expert testimony inadmissible under Daubert standards for failing to establish general causation (i.e., no reliable evidence acetaminophen can cause ASD in humans).^[5]^
